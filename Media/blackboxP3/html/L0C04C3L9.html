Where does coronavirus come from?Strange theory suggests from space

    Ever since the coronavirus outbreak plunged the world into a crisis, researchers have sprung into action to find its origin, device a quick test to detect it and develop an effective vaccine at the earliest.

   Almost a year into the novel coronavirus pandemic and yet, the source of the virus is yet to be confirmed.Some say it was 'developed' at China's Wuhan lab, while some have argued that it's a bat-borne virus.

   But, a study published in July this year suggests a hard-to-believe idea that the virus came from space and had reached Earth on a space rock or a meteor.

   The research paper analyses the origins of two recently emergent epidemics: a fungal disease caused by Candida auris and the current Covid-19 pandemic.

   'These two epidemics display distinctive features and clear evidence that they may have come from a space in-fall of infectious viruses and micro-organisms in cometary dust or meteorite-derived dust particles,' the paper reads.

   The study conducted by a group of researchers including Edward J Steele and N Chandra Wickramasinghe explains this space virus theory in detail, stating that the virus was dispersed over China, the epicenter of the pandemic when a meteor fell towards Earth in October.

    A report in Express.co.uk quoted astrobiologist Chandra Wickramasinghe as saying that: 'The sudden outbreak of a new coronavirus is very likely to have a space connection.'

   'A VIRAL BOMB EXPLOSION'

    Emphasising on the unique coronavirus-origin theory, the study explained an 'alternate hypothesis' that Covid-19 arrived on a space rock -- 'via a meteorite, a presumed relatively fragile and loose carbonaceous meteorite' -- which was struck North-East China on October 11, 2019.

   It noted that 'an exceptionally bright fireball event' was seen on that day.

   'A reasonable assumption is that the fireball which struck 2000 km N of Wuhan may have been part of a wide tube of debris the bulk of which was deposited in the stratosphere to fall over Wuhan,' it said.

   'We then assume the viral debris and particles then made landfall in Wuhan and related regions about a month to 6 weeks later resulting in first cases of viral pneumonia caused by Covid-19 emerging in Wuhan regions late November 2019-early December 2019,' the paper read.

   The researchers said that ' it [the outbreak] actually looks like a huge viral bomb explosion took place near or over Wuhan'.

   It 'then the radial fall-out of the disease-causing viral particles to land on the millions of people either laterally or from above,' they said.

   This idea, how much ever strange and wild it appears, is not a novel one.

   A report in Express.co.uk said that this space virus theory is known as panspermia (in astronomy) - 'the theory that life is being spread across the universe on fast-moving asteroids and comets'.

   CORONAVIRUS, A BAT BORNE VIRUS?

    'The theory that it jumped from bats via snakes to humans is implausible,' the researchers said.

   Initially, findings had revealed that the coronavirus had emerged in a sea-food market in China's Wuhan.

   However, the research paper points out that 'many cases of disease [about 30 per cent of case reports] arose in locations unconnected with the Wuhan seafood and meat market'.

   The paper further said that the initial explanation that Covid-19 jumped from bats (possibly via snakes) to humans and then spread via human-to-human contact is 'at odds with the data at present'.

   'THEORY IS UNLIKELY'

    The theory is unlikely -- an astrobiologist, Graham Lau, had told Space.com in an interview.

   Calling the theory as an 'extraordinary claim', Lau said that even though the idea is interesting, 'we just don't have any reason to embrace' it right now.

   Lau explained that there might be a reason to investigate the possibility of extraterrestrial origins of the virus 'if this thing had some different kind of biomolecule in it that's different from life as we know it'.

   However, even in that case, there could be Earthly explanations, Lau said.

   WHO SAYS CORONAVIRUS IS NATURAL IN ORIGIN

    Amid the conspiracy theories, the World Health Organization (WHO) has maintained that the novel coronavirus is 'natural in origin'.

   Dr Michael Ryan had earlier said that WHO teams have listened 'again and again' to many scientists who have looked at the gene sequences and the virus, 'and we are assured that this virus is natural in origin.'
